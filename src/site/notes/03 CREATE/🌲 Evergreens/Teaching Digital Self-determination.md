# Teaching Digital Self-determination

When you ask people to change the tools they use every day, you're not just changing software. You're making a claim about who owns their digital life — and that claim is curriculum.

Tool migration from surveillance platforms to privacy-respecting alternatives becomes one of the most powerful teaching opportunities available, precisely because it makes abstract ideas concrete. Questions about power, ownership, and community governance stop being theoretical the moment someone has to move their files from Google Drive to [[03 CREATE/🪴 Groves/Privacy by Design\|CryptPad]].

---

## Beyond Technical Training

Traditional technology training asks: *How do I use this tool?*

Critical digital pedagogy asks the harder questions:

- **Why** does this tool exist, and who funded it?
- **Who** benefits when our community uses it?
- **How** does this platform shape our relationships and habits?
- **What** alternatives reflect our actual values?

These aren't rhetorical questions. They have real answers — and walking through them with learners builds the kind of critical consciousness that outlasts any single tool choice.

## The Teaching Framework

Four ideas work together to make this approach work in practice:

**[[03 CREATE/🌲 Evergreens/Digital Tool Choice as Values Expression\|Digital Tool Choice as Values Expression]]** — Every platform decision teaches something, whether you intend it to or not. Making that teaching explicit is the move. When communities choose between surveillance capitalism and cooperative ownership, between centralized control and federation, they're practicing [[03 CREATE/🌲 Evergreens/Digital Sovereignty\|Digital Sovereignty]] — not just swapping apps.

**[[03 CREATE/🌲 Evergreens/Tool Migration as Pedagogy\|Tool Migration as Pedagogy]]** — Migration projects aren't disruptions to learning. They *are* the learning. The friction of moving real documents, setting up real communications, and making real governance decisions teaches things a workshop never could.

**[[03 CREATE/🌲 Evergreens/Tiered Technical Scaffolding Model\|Tiered Technical Scaffolding Model]]** — Most communities have a wide range of technical comfort. A tiered model — core champions, power users, general users — prevents the failure mode where the most confident people make all the decisions and everyone else gets left behind.

**[[03 CREATE/🌲 Evergreens/Harm Reduction in Digital Literacy\|Harm Reduction in Digital Literacy]]** — No perfect tools exist. Any pedagogy that ignores tradeoffs sets people up for failure. Sustainable digital self-determination is built on incremental progress and realistic expectations, not purity.

---

## What Success Looks Like

Rather than individual test scores, the meaningful measures are collective:

- Can the community manage its own infrastructure through a staff transition?
- Can members support each other through technical challenges without depending on a single expert?
- Do people participate in decisions about which tools the community uses?
- Do tool choices actually reflect the community's stated values?

These are harder to measure than quiz scores. They're also what actually matters.
