## üìö Read

** World's top Internet companies and telcos rated on protection of users' digital rights (http://arstechnica.co.uk/tech-policy/2015/11/worlds-top-internet-companies-and-telcos-rated-on-protection-of-users-digital-rights/)

In the ** Post-Snowden era (http://www.pewinternet.org/2014/11/12/public-privacy-perceptions/)

, we're increasingly concerned/aware of our privacy and security in online and hybrid spaces. The ** Ranking Digital Rights (https://rankingdigitalrights.org/)

** 2015 Corporate Accountability Index (https://rankingdigitalrights.org/index2015/)

examines corporate practices and how they affect freedom of expression and privacy.

In the rankings Google scored best among Internet companies while Facebook lagged behind. In this work, it is important to determine and routinely question the values we hold dear in online spaces...and whether or not companies have these same belief systems.

** (http://techcrunch.com/2015/06/26/molson-and-google-built-a-beer-fridge-that-unlocks-via-voice-translation/?ncid=rss)

** Addressing Ageism in the 21st Century Classroom (http://www.hybridpedagogy.com/journal/addressing-ageism-in-the-21st-century-classroom/)

Great post from ** Joanna Murphy (https://twitter.com/JoMamaMurphy)

at ** Hybrid Pedagogy (http://www.hybridpedagogy.com/)

looking at the role of power, authority, and truth as we consider age in teaching and learning. Many times we focus on gender or race in examinations of literacy and technology use in teaching. Rarely in my experience do we consider the permutations of ageism and paying attention to power dynamics in and out of the classroom.

Joanna indicates that one of the first steps is examining our own beliefs and notions about these factors.

In our positions of power, we have the ability to shift discourses surrounding our young(er) students and become more aware of how we speak about them and what ‚Äúisms‚Äù we might be enacting with and outside of our classrooms.

** The digital disparities facing lower-income teenagers (http://bits.blogs.nytimes.com/2015/11/03/the-digital-disparities-facing-lower-income-teens/)

In a report by ** Common Sense Media (https://www.commonsensemedia.org/)

they indicate that only one-fourth of teenagers in houses with less than $35,000 in annual income say that they had their own laptops as compared to 62 percent in households with annual incomes of $100,000 or more.

This news is even more disconcerting when compared to ** research (http://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/store/10.1002/rrq.85/asset/rrq85.pdf?v=1&t=ignstlks&s=f2c17f7221c2c0eadb2d400b4a355d6ff0eaf2fd&systemMessage=Wiley+Online+Library+will+be+unavailable+on+Saturday+7th+November+2015++from+10%3A00-16%3A00+GMT+%2F+05%3A00-11%3A00+EST+%2F+18%3A00-00%3A00+SGT+for+essential+maintenance.++Apologies+for+the+inconvenience.)

conducted by the ** New Lit Lab (http://newliteracies.uconn.edu/)

that indicated that in our schools students that need literacy and technology instruction might not be receiving it.

Put simply, these web literacies are fundamental human rights that all of our students need. It seems that the ones that might need it the most, aren't accessing it at home or school.

** (http://www.psfk.com/2015/06/google-eavesdropping-tool-audio-snooping-code-privacy.html)

** Deliberate Practice and Digital Literacies (http://dmlcentral.net/deliberate-practice-and-digital-literacies/)

In this piece by ** Doug Belshaw (https://twitter.com/dajbelshaw)

on ** DML Central (http://dmlcentral.net/)

he discusses possibilities for developing an individual's digital literacies through deliberate practice. In this we embed opportunities to practice and build up facility with digital literacies over time with learners. By focusing on granular skills, we have the opportunity to turn these discrete skills into habits.

I definitely agree with Doug's thinking in this post. I think it's also something (as he indicates) in the post that we need to start at an early age. I spend time with students in Pre-K through three working on LED hacks, and might think about coding clubs as he has indicated. In this I not only bring awareness and instruction to tiny tykes...but also build up my own skillset.

** (http://www.wired.com/2015/06/lexus-hoverboard-slide/)

** The top six things organizations must do to enable emergent learning (http://idreflections.blogspot.ca/2015/11/the-top-six-things-organizations-must.html)

Intriguing overview on emergent learning in organizations, but I think this post contains guidance on enabling and empowering learners in all areas. The focus on a "** growth mindset (https://www.brainpickings.org/2014/01/29/carol-dweck-mindset/)

" has been in fashion for the last year or so following ** Carol Dweck's book (http://www.amazon.com/Mindset-The-New-Psychology-Success/dp/0345472322/?tag=braipick-20)

.

This post identifies six key elements:

1. Shift from networks to communities

2. Give up hierarchical, command and control mindset

3. Make employees (learner) engagement an outcome, not the goal

4. Make the purpose bigger than shareholder value creation

5. Stop viewing individuals as replaceable resources

6. Celebrate diversity in all aspects - cognitive and otherwise

** (http://www.wired.com/2015/06/lexus-hoverboard-slide/)

In my house we have several tablets, old phones, and computers from family members, or old purchases that we've moved on from. Most of these are used as gaming devices for my son, quick cameras for classroom use and demos...or waiting to get hacked into something new that we'll use.

One such device is an ** old Nexus 7 (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nexus_7_(2012))

that I've been searching for a new purpose. My initial thought was to use it as an alarm clock that I would use in the guest bedroom. This would be helpful for family/friends that stay over as it would be loaded with maps, apps for the house, and the remote to operate the TV. I'm thinking instead that we'll build this "smart mirror" using the Nexus and the ** directions from Adafruit (https://learn.adafruit.com/android-smart-home-mirror)

. It'll be a fun project that I can work on with my son as we both learn how to hack new things from old.
