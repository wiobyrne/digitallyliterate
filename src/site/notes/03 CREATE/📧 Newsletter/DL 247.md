### [Planning for Fall 2020 in Higher Education](https://bryanalexander.org/scenarios/two-competing-visions-of-fall-higher-education-plus-a-ghostly-third/)

As the academic year closes for most institutions from K-12 through higher ed, our collective focus turns to the next opening in the Fall.

Bryan Alexander continues to [carefully examine the possibilities](https://bryanalexander.org/scenarios/two-competing-visions-of-fall-higher-education-plus-a-ghostly-third/) as we plan the fall of 2020 in higher ed. This future may include [teaching behind plexiglass](https://www.edsurge.com/news/2020-05-22-teaching-behind-plexiglass-colleges-wrestle-with-details-of-resuming-in-person-classes) as we strive to protect all individuals. There are also questions about the [erosion of trust and leadership in our universities](https://www.chronicle.com/article/University-Leaders-Are-Failing/248809) that has been laid bare in our current milieu.

Parents and learners in Pre-K through 12 are asking the purpose of online learning, and whether we should be focused on the [mental health of children](https://www.forbes.com/sites/nickmorrison/2020/05/15/its-not-childrens-education-we-should-worry-about-its-their-mental-health/) and [teachers](https://thejournal.com/articles/2020/05/19/caring-for-educators-is-the-first-step-in-serving-students.aspx).

[Mark Lilla](https://marklilla.com/) suggests that we should stop asking pundits and prophets to predict the future. [It doesn't exist](https://www.nytimes.com/2020/05/22/opinion/coronavirus-prediction-future.html). It will exist only after we have made it.

One common voice in this discussion is the refrain of the classroom teacher that once again steps up to do the most with the fewest resources. This [post by Harley Litzelman](https://thebolditalic.com/we-cannot-return-to-campus-this-fall-1ad91b8a65e0) details the spatial, logistical, pedagogical, disciplinary, and epidemiological nightmare into which non-teaching decision-makers are welcoming your children.

### [The Information Apocalypse Is Already Here, And Reality Is Losing](https://www.buzzfeednews.com/article/craigsilverman/coronavirus-information-apocalypse)

We've spent more than three years preparing for an information apocalypse. It seems like with the coronavirus we're only seeing these trends accelerate.

As it turns out, the tools needed to unmoor people from our shared reality already exist and are less technological than societal.

This [post](https://www.politifact.com/article/2020/may/22/how-fact-check-coronavirus-misinformation-your-tim/) identifies some guidance on how to fact-check misinfo on your timeline:

- Don't brush it off
- Consider your approach
- Watch your languageâ€”or don't
- Avoid repeating misinformation. Say what's true
- Choose your sources wisely
- Focus on facts, not values

While on this topic, there is debate about the [real statistics](https://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2020/04/21/world/coronavirus-missing-deaths.html) around COVID-19. The [COVID Tracking Project](https://covidtracking.com/) released a [white paper](https://covidtracking.com/documents/CDC_Report_CTP.pdf) that compiles the latest numbers on tests, confirmed cases, hospitalizations, and patient outcomes from every US state and territory.

A recent study gleaned five insights on virtual instruction by examining the techniques shared in common by top-rated online instructors:

1. Authentic and relevant course material
2. A variety of multimedia resources
3. Student creation of contentâ€”individually and collaboratively
4. Student reflection on learning
5. Explanation of purpose

### [As Machines Get Smarter, How Will We Relate to Them?](https://www.wired.com/story/as-machines-get-smarter-how-will-we-relate-to-them/)

Much of the Internet has become unintelligible lately. What this means is that it is almost impossible to understand the algorithms and pipelines that connect us to the digital. This will become even more of a challenge as we consider artificial intelligence (AI), drones, and autonomous vehicles.

As we're all sequestered in our homes, it seems like [drones were ready for this moment](https://www.nytimes.com/2020/05/23/style/drones-coronavirus.html) and are [watching us](https://www.wired.com/story/proxemo-robot-guesses-emotion-from-walking/).

We need to regularly think about these [moral crumple zones](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=2757236) in our livesâ€”the places where humans absorb blame when algorithmic systems fail.
