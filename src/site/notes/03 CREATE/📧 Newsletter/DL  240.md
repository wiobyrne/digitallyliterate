### [The Fog of Pandemic](https://www.nytimes.com/)

We've grown accustomed to living through an information war fought largely by hardened political operatives and trolls.

While the coronavirus crisis is political and will continue to be politicized, its most consequential fights will take place in the "fog of pandemic" where so much of our dataâ€”from health statistics to economic indicatorsâ€”is flawed or evolving. Today's propaganda could be tomorrow's truth. Or vice versa. Even the good guys are working with limited information and hoping for the best. We are not prepared for what's coming.

As this information spreads, [cell phone data shows people are not social distancing](https://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2020/04/02/us/coronavirus-social-distancing.html), especially in [certain regions of the U.S.](https://www.theatlantic.com/politics/archive/2020/04/coronavirus-unique-threat-south-young-people/609241/)

### [How to Talk With Kids About Screentime and COVID-19](https://www.theverge.com/2020/3/31/21201807/children-adolescents-screentime-coronavirus-how-to-psychiatrist)

There is discussion that the [screentime debate has ended, and screens won](https://www.nytimes.com/2020/03/31/technology/coronavirus-screen-time.html). I don't entirely agree with this, but I have been more thoughtful about our use of screens as we adapt. I reflect on this regularly in our [Technopanic Podcast](https://screentime.me/technopanic-podcast/). Please don't let it [interfere with sleep patterns](https://www.edutopia.org/article/pediatricians-balancing-screen-time-sleep-and-family-during-coronavirus).

It's important to recognize that youth are trying to make sense of these contexts and practices. If you're looking for guidance, this resource from [Learning in Places](http://learninginplaces.org/for-families/) is excellent.

### ['Zoombombing' Is a Federal Offense That Could Result in Imprisonment](https://www.theverge.com/2020/4/3/21207260/zoombombing-crime-zoom-video-conference-hacking-pranks-doj-fbi)

[Zoombombing](https://www.nytimes.com/2020/04/03/technology/zoom-harassment-abuse-racism-fbi-warning.html) is when someone successfully invades a public or sometimes even private meeting over the videoconferencing platform to broadcast shock videos, pornography, or other disruptive content.

This happened to a couple of my colleagues this week.

Prosecutors say they'll pursue charges including "disrupting a public meeting, computer intrusion, using a computer to commit a crime, hate crimes, fraud, or transmitting threatening communications." Some charges include fines and possible imprisonment.

If you or anyone you know becomes a victim, report it to the [FBI's Internet Crime Complaint Center](https://www.ic3.gov/default.aspx).

### [Make and Wear a Cloth Face Mask](https://www.cdc.gov/coronavirus/2019-ncov/prevent-getting-sick/cloth-face-cover.html)

The [CDC now recommends](https://www.cdc.gov/coronavirus/2019-ncov/prevent-getting-sick/cloth-face-cover.html) cloth face coverings in public.

[Research suggests](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41591-020-0843-2) surgical face masks could prevent transmission of human coronaviruses and influenza from symptomatic individuals. Perhaps [we should all be wearing them](https://medium.com/@Cancerwarrior/covid-19-why-we-should-all-wear-masks-there-is-new-scientific-rationale-280e08ceee71).

My family has a certain amount of [privilege](https://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2020/04/03/us/coronavirus-stay-home-rich-poor.html) and can shelter in place. We leave once a week for groceries. When we do leave, we wear masks to show solidarity with workers who need to be out in stores and markets. It is not about us. It is about the people who need to be out working.

Got a T-shirt? You can [make a mask at home](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9yP_fnr4oVY&feature=youtu.be).

## ðŸ¤” Consider

### When another person makes you suffer, it is because he suffers deeply within himself, and his suffering is spilling over. He does not need punishment; he needs help.

*Thich Nhat Hanh*

Thich Nhat Hanh's teaching on compassion connects to this issue's theme of care ethics. Whether it's Zoombombers disrupting classes or regions resisting social distancing, responding with punishment alone misses the deeper suffering. An ethics of care asks what help is needed, not just what rules were broken.
