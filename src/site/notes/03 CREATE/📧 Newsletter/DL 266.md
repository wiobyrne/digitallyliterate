Universities are digitally spying on students to make sure they don‚Äôt cheat on online tests. A whole generation could be learning to tolerate surveillance.

Proctoring software is a symptom of a deeper mistake: Using tech to manage a problem that is fundamentally economic.

Many students are now being asked to use remote-proctoring services like ProctorU, ExamSoft, or Proctorio when taking exams online. While these services were around before the pandemic, they have grown in popularity as the number of students taking classes remotely exploded.

In theory, they provide a useful service, allowing professors or universities to determine whether students are cheating on exams. In practice, these services have been a huge source of stress for students.

Many students who are required per their teacher‚Äôs instructions to take tests while being recorded told Mary Retta (https://twitter.com/mary__retta) at Teen Vogue (https://www.teenvogue.com/story/exam-surveillance-tools-remote-learning) they felt heavily surveilled while doing so.

** High Tech, High Risk: Tech Ethics Lessons for the COVID-19 Pandemic Response (https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666389920301367)

------------------------------------------------------------

As the COVID-19 pandemic has drastically reshaped society around the world, many have looked to machine learning as a technology capable of addressing large problems at scale. Applications of machine learning has been seen as desirable interventions in mitigating the risks of the pandemic disease.

The challenge is that machine learning, like many tools of technocratic governance, is deeply implicated in the social production and distribution of risk.

Emanuel Moss (https://twitter.com/MannyMoss) and Jacob Metcalf (https://twitter.com/undersequoias) on the role of machine learning in the production of risk must be considered as engineers and other technologists develop tools for the current crisis.

** Family Device Rules (https://fosstodon.org/@dajbelshaw/105105994421635977)

------------------------------------------------------------

Doug Belshaw (https://dougbelshaw.com/) with some great guidance on how to discuss and agree on device rules.

1. Wrote down (individually) what we think the current rules are. Had a discussion.

2. Wrote down (individually) what we think the future rules should be. Had another discussion.

3. Collaboratively came up with rules that everyone could agree to. Signed them, and pinned to fridge.
