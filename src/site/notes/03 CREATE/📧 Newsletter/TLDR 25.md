### [The Internet's loop of action and reaction is worsening](http://www.nytimes.com/2015/12/10/technology/shut-down-internet-donald-trump-hillary-clinton.html?partner=rss&emc=rss)

A piece from [Farhad Manjoo](https://twitter.com/fmanjoo) in the NY Times that starts by recounting some recent reactions to political events here in the U.S. and then extends to the online discourse and norms.

The online space has become a cycle of action, reaction, and kneejerk decisions.

Farhad states:

The spiraling feedback loop is exhausting and rarely illuminating. The news brims with instantly produced "hot takes" and a raft of fact-free assertions. Everyone â€” yours truly included â€” is always on guard for the next opportunity to meme-ify outrage: What crazy thing did Trump/Obama/The New York Times/The New York Post/Rush Limbaugh/etc. say now, and what clever quip can you fit into a tweet to quickly begin collecting likes?

### [How Facebook learns while you forget: The science behind social media nostalgia](http://blogs.scientificamerican.com/mind-guest-blog/how-facebook-learns-while-you-forget/)

A blog post from [Dr. Julia Shaw](http://www.drjuliashaw.com/) on the [Scientific American](http://www.scientificamerican.com/) blog.

She discusses the challenges of personal memories and the challenges that arise as social media and algorithms manipulate nostalgia. Specifically she is referencing Facebook's "on this day" feature and the same type of product offered by [Timehop](http://timehop.com/).

Julia includes:

Facebook has even taken it upon itself to appropriate the very term 'memory', classifying things as 'memories' only if they were posted on its site. It seems to be not-so-subtly implying that if something wasn't posted on Facebook it may as well have not happened at all.

For some more perspective...check out [this piece on the PsyBlog](http://www.spring.org.uk/2015/12/one-more-curious-way-the-internet-has-changed-how-we-think.php) that discusses how the ways in which the "Internet" might be changing the ways we think and view our own knowledge.

### [What is an algorithm?](https://www.commoncraft.com/video-algorithms-what-they-are-why-they-matter)

When we discuss coding and programming, much of this talk centers on patterns and problem-solving using/developing algorithms. In recent writing and thinking about coding, I indicate that perhaps the real goal is to have students problem-solve using algorithmic thinking. The challenge is...what is an algorithm.

This video from [Common Craft](https://www.commoncraft.com/blog) is another great resource to add to the collection. Have students watch this and think of others ways algorithms are or could be used in their lives.

### [Some useful Google Photos tips for teachers](http://www.educatorstechnology.com/2015/12/useful-google-photos-tips-for-teachers.html)

I'll admit that I use Google apps tools probably a bit too much. I try and diversify the tools that I use, and make sure I use other tools or backups in case I lose (or Google loses) something.

One tool that I use daily (and love) is Google Photos. [Google Photos](https://photos.google.com/) will allow you to automatically upload photos from your computer, or [mobile device](https://support.google.com/photos/answer/6193313?hl=en).

You can also use this tool to [make basic edits in photos](https://support.google.com/photos/answer/6128850?hl=en). For something more advanced, [Snapseed](http://iphonephotographyschool.com/snapseed-tutorial/) is available on [iOs](https://www.google.com/url?sa=t&rct=j&q=&esrc=s&source=web&cd=3&ved=0ahUKEwi6g8XiydTJAhUJ7iYKHQ39BpIQFggrMAI&url=https%3A%2F%2Fitunes.apple.com%2Fus%2Fapp%2Fsnapseed%2Fid439438619%3Fmt%3D8&usg=AFQjCNGf-CuUmIuANMsmXhZyX1bKNXeAig&sig2=Pr-TOFBhK9XIou0TobsOhA)/[Android](https://www.google.com/url?sa=t&rct=j&q=&esrc=s&source=web&cd=1&ved=0ahUKEwi6g8XiydTJAhUJ7iYKHQ39BpIQFggdMAA&url=https%3A%2F%2Fplay.google.com%2Fstore%2Fapps%2Fdetails%3Fid%3Dcom.niksoftware.snapseed%26hl%3Den&usg=AFQjCNEnP8P-W6M0LHfd5156TisN9aNl-w&sig2=rGsU84ZaAdqar6zJhBEZZQ).
