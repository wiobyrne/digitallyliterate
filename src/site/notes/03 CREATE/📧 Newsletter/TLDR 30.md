### [How "moxie" can help students succeed](http://www.edutopia.org/blog/how-moxie-helps-students-succeed-jennifer-levin-goldberg)

Over the past couple of years we've heard more talk about mindsets, soft skills, and dispositions. Within these discussions, we've already read a great deal about the growth mindset, resilience, or determination.

In this post from [Edutopia](http://www.edutopia.org/), [Jennifer Goldberg](https://plus.google.com/u/0/110640267092114891939) posits that students need to build/possess positivity, purpose, and volition.

### [Where do the world's highest paid teachers live?](https://talk.chalk.com/the-most-and-least-paid-teachers-in-the-world-76e312dc2cd1#.afhziyi5m)

[Tom Ostapchuk](https://twitter.com/TomOstapchuk) pulled data from the OECD's report titled ["Education at a Glance: OECD Indicators"](http://www.oecd-ilibrary.org/education/education-at-a-glance-2015/indicator-d3-how-much-are-teachers-paid_eag-2015-32-en;jsessionid=4nvdgubgnaife.x-oecd-live-02) and created graphical comparisons of teacher pay across nations.

Tom also pulled out some (equally) interesting info about [teacher pay and recent trends](https://talk.chalk.com/teachers-in-new-york-earn-90-more-than-in-south-dakota-56421747a7b1#.a2hj2s7lo) across US States.

Perhaps teacher salary isn't the only indicator...but perhaps we need to consider how we value education and educators as we identify new ways to evaluate them.

### [Guide to using Twitter in your teaching practice](http://ww2.kqed.org/education/how-to-use-twitter-in-your-teaching-practice/)

A great resource produced by [KQED Education](http://www.kqed.org/education/) and the [Trust and Safety Team at Twitter](https://about.twitter.com/safety).

The post provides granular advice and support on online safety, netiquette, Twitter 101, parent/administrator resources, and digital media formats.

### [Spotify's design lead on why side projects should be stupid](http://firstround.com/review/Spotifys-Design-Lead-on-Why-Side-Projects-Should-be-Stupid/)

An interview with Tobias van Schneider in [FirstRound Review](http://firstround.com/review/#rt_e=eyJjYW1wYWlnbiI6IkhlYWRlciJ9) provides guidance as you consider side projects...or taking a risk in your work. This post was much-needed advice in my own world.

We hear educators consider implementing the [20% time rule from Google](http://www.businessinsider.com/google-20-percent-time-policy-2015-4) into their own classroom routine. Within this context, Tobias offers some advice for your own students...or your upcoming side projects.

- Let yourself be stupid.
- Take it one step at a time.
- Ditch your obsession with growth.
- Trust yourself more.

### [A scientific theory of humor](http://www.scientificamerican.com/article/a-scientific-theory-of-humor/)

A post from [Scientific American](http://www.scientificamerican.com/) looking at research trying to unpack the formula behind being funny.

I view humor as a tool in my own life and practice. The problem is that humor is often viewed differently...but different individuals. Perhaps there is a method or formula behind what people perceive as funny.

For now...I'll stick to my self-deprecating style and hope to not "put my foot in my own mouth" for at least another day.

## ðŸ”¨ Do

### [Harvard University's Berkman Center releases Amber, a tool to help keep the Web available](https://cyber.law.harvard.edu/node/99276)

If you're a regular blogger....or have just started up a new website...one of the challenges is that many of the links that we connect to in our posts frequently become disconnected, fail, go down...or just rot.

This happened to me in class this week as students tried to click on some links I left behind in class materials.

[Amber](http://amberlink.org/) is a tool that archives links and creates snapshots of pages that you've linked to. If the page is not available...Amber will recognize this and send the reader to an archived version of the intended page. Brilliant.
