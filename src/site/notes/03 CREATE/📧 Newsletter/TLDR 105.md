### [Writing Wikipedia articles teaches information literacy skills, study finds](https://blog.wikimedia.org/2017/06/19/wikipedia-information-literacy-study/)

Many times in our classrooms, we indicate that learners should not use Wikipedia because "anyone can write anything" on the site. My response to this is to show people the [Talk pages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:Introduction_to_talk_pages/1) on Wikipedia articles, and the [robust debate/discussion that should happen there](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=BU5Ga_Yz8ZU).

This post from the Wikimedia Education Foundation (Wiki Ed) shares findings from research that investigated learning gains from courses in which students were required to edit Wikipedia articles. Findings suggest gains in understanding of digital literacy (online trust, critical evaluation of online information), as well as basic information and media literacy skills. The [full report is here](https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/File:Student_Learning_Outcomes_using_Wikipedia-based_Assignments_Fall_2016_Research_Report.pdf).

From my own experience as a student, I've enjoyed and profited from classes in the past that required me to edit Wikipedia articles. In my classes I have students edit a course wiki that we set up in Wikispaces. I may just have them edit/review content on Wikipedia.

### [How to inspire a group of people to do the impossible](https://www.inc.com/lolly-daskal/how-to-inspire-a-group-of-people-to-do-the-impossi.html)

I've been doing some thinking about "leadership" over the past year or so and trying to identify how this impacts my thinking and practice.

This post from [Lolly Daskal](https://twitter.com/LollyDaskal) on [Inc](https://www.inc.com/). shares 10 ways you can inspire others:
- Make it meaningful
- Communicate clearly
- Pick the right people with the right skills
- Encourage strength through unity
- Celebrate the small wins that drive big progress
- Provide the support they need
- Cultivate a climate of trust
- Don't discourage failure
- Anticipate discouragement
- Focus on possibilities, not problems

### [7 critical skills for the jobs of the future](https://singularityhub.com/2017/07/04/7-critical-skills-for-the-jobs-of-the-future/)

One of the regular topics here in TL;DR over the past six months has been a look at the "future of jobs" as technology, education, and automation impact society. This is a big topic as we see economies changing, and this in turn impacts our political systems.

This post shares insight from a [World Economic Forum report](http://reports.weforum.org/future-of-jobs-2016/chapter-1-the-future-of-jobs-and-skills/) I previously shared in TL;DR about the future of jobs. Tony Wagner shares the [seven survival skills](http://www.tonywagner.com/7-survival-skills) needed now and in the future:
- Critical thinking & problem solving
- Collaboration across networks & leading by influence
- Agility & adaptability
- Initiative & entrepreneurship
- Effective oral & written communication
- Assessing & analyzing information
- Curiosity & imagination

### [Why I've just ditched my cloud-based password manager](http://dougbelshaw.com/blog/2017/07/06/lastpass-to-lesspass/)

I've frequently shared advice about password managers, and specifically [LastPass](https://lastpass.com/), as you secure your digital identity. [Doug Belshaw](https://twitter.com/dajbelshaw) shared this post in his awesome [Thought Shrapnel newsletter](http://thoughtshrapnel.com/) about his thoughts as he is moving from Lastpass to [Lesspass](https://lesspass.com/).

I've been worried about Lastpass over the past year, and have been thinking about overhauling my password system. I think I'll follow Belshaw's lead and move to Lesspass once I see how it works for him.

Also, if you haven't already, check out [Thought Shrapnel Live](https://telegram.me/thoughtshrapnel). Doug is using Telegram to regularly update you with important things he's finding throughout the day.
