### [Why writers run](http://www.theatlantic.com/entertainment/archive/2015/11/why-writers-run/415146/)

Now that I've given you a ton of things to go listen to...you might be wondering what else to do during that time.

This piece by [Nick Ripatrazone](https://twitter.com/nickripatrazone) on [The Atlantic](http://www.theatlantic.com/) is about much more than writers running. To me it was about creativity, inspiration, and creating quiet in the mind to make that happen.

I've blogged in the past about my own [30 day challenge](http://wiobyrne.com/the-30-day-challenge/) (that has become a habit) [with meditation](http://wiobyrne.com/meditation-mindfulness-gaining-headspace-in-my-workflow/). I think we need to find opportunities to clear our heads out, calm the mind, and let the wonderous machine that is our brain do its work.

If you're looking at other opportunities to clear out your own head...I recommend checking out [Laura Hilliger's](https://twitter.com/epilepticrabbit) [post on structure and routine](https://medium.com/the-slackerati/don-t-be-so-negative-6572d4c31a02).

### [How video games can teach your brain to fight depression](http://www.slate.com/articles/technology/future_tense/2015/11/how_video_games_can_teach_your_brain_to_fight_depression.html)

This Slate article by [Jane McGonigal](https://twitter.com/avantgame) discusses the benefits of gaming, and specifically a "gameful" mindset as you approach daily challenges.

You can learn more by checking out the [SuperBetter](https://www.superbetter.com/) [book](http://www.amazon.com/dp/1594206368/?tag=slatmaga-20) and app. Or, as I learned from her [interview on the Tim Ferriss podcast](http://fourhourworkweek.com/2015/07/28/jane-mcgonigal/)...install Tetris on your phone and play it for five minutes at the end of the night. I play [Tetroid on Android](https://play.google.com/store/apps/details?id=com.oblius.tetroid&hl=en) as I've had troubles finding a good Tetris app that isn't junked up with ads and fluff.

While we're discussing the positive potential of games to heal...there is [new research](http://www.researchgate.net/publication/280294047_Challenging_games_help_students_learn_An_empirical_study_on_engagement_flow_and_immersion_in_game-based_learning) out looking at the opportunity to [increase engagement and help students learn through challenging games](http://gamification-research.org/2015/11/challenging/).

### [The digital revolution in higher education has already happened. No one noticed.](https://medium.com/@cshirky/the-digital-revolution-in-higher-education-has-already-happened-no-one-noticed-78ec0fec16c7)

Great piece by [Clay Shirky](https://twitter.com/cshirky) in which he discusses the new normal...online education is a fundamental part of our experience in higher ed. The challenge is that it appears that we haven't put that much thought into what it could, or should be.

He states:

Given the lousy fit between institutional assumptions and the actual lives of most students, we should applaud their inventiveness in using digital options to make college work for them. But we should also recognize our complicity in creating a system that works so badly in the first place. Online classes are no longer surprising, or experimental, or rare. By adopting them, students are telling us what they need our institutions to become.

I agree with the points that he makes. I am concerned for what is happening in K-12 education.

### [A different approach to coding](https://medium.com/bright/a-different-approach-to-coding-d679b06d83a)

This post by Mitch Resnick and David Siegel, students have tremendous opportunities to make and remake themselves as they learn to code. In the post they provide some framing for the [2015 Hour of Code](https://hourofcode.com/us) that will be lunched by [Code.org](https://code.org/).

They discuss a need to understand the motivations and perspectives that promote this focus on coding and programming. They view it as more of a literacy as opposed to preparing workers for the future.

In the post they state:

As young people express themselves with Scratch, they begin to think of themselves differently, just as they do when they learn to write. The Brazilian educator Paulo Freire recognized that writing is more than just a practical skill. He led literacy campaigns in poor communities not simply to help people get jobs, but to help people learn that, in his words, "they can make and remake themselves."
