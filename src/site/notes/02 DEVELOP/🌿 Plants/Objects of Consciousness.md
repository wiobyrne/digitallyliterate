# Objects of Consciousness

The study of the "objects of consciousness" sits at the intersection of phenomenology, cognitive science, and digital philosophy. To understand consciousness, we must first understand that it is almost always _about_ something. In philosophy, this is known as intentionality. We don't just "conscious"; we are conscious of a thought, a sensation, a memory, or the glowing screen of a laptop. These are the objects that populate our internal theater.

## The Construction of the Object

A common "miracle" we accept in daily life is the assumption that the objects we perceive are direct, unmediated reflections of reality. However, data science and neuroscience suggest otherwise. What we perceive as a solid objectâ€”say, a Jeep Wrangler or a printed bookâ€”is actually a high-level representation constructed by the brain from a chaotic stream of sensory "pipes."

The brain acts as a data scientist, taking raw, noisy inputs (photons, pressure waves) and running them through a predictive model to create a stable "object." We don't see the world as it is; we see a "satisficed" version of the world that is stable enough for us to navigate. The object of consciousness is a model, and as we have discussed, all models are provisional.

## Digital Objects and the Extended Mind

In the modern era, the nature of these objects has shifted. We now spend hours interacting with digital objects: Markdown files, lines of code, or nodes in an Obsidian graph. These aren't physical, yet they occupy the same space in our consciousness as a physical tool.

When you build a knowledge vault, you are essentially architecting the objects of your future consciousness. By organizing your digital life, you are choosing which data points will be "extracted" and "transformed" into the thoughts you have tomorrow. This is the essence of the Extended Mind Thesis: our tools are not just external to us; they are the scaffolding upon which our conscious objects are built.

## The Subject-Object Divide

A primary challenge in "doing science" on consciousness is the difficulty of turning the subject (the "I") into an object of study. When we try to observe our own consciousness, we are like a flashlight trying to turn around and see its own light source.

If we follow the scientific attitude of "assuming we are wrong," we must consider that our sense of a unified "self" might be just another object of consciousnessâ€”a useful fiction or a "miracle" of biology designed to keep the organism coherent. In this view, the "I" is not the observer of the objects; it is simply the most persistent object in the stream.

## Conclusion: Sovereignty Over the Object

Understanding that our consciousness is populated by models and representations gives us a unique form of sovereignty. If the objects of our awareness are constructed, we have a say in how that construction happens. We can choose to look past the "black box" of the interfaces we use and understand the underlying logic.

By curating our "pipes"â€”the information we consume, the books we read, and the digital systems we buildâ€”we are not just organizing data. We are deliberately selecting the objects that will define our conscious experience. We move from being passive observers of a mental movie to being the directors of our own cognitive territory.
