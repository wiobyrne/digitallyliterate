# Communication as Organization

In the traditional sociological view, communication was the "voice" of an organizationâ€”a tool used by a pre-existing entity to recruit members or broadcast a platform. However, in the age of connective action, the thesis has flipped: **Communication is the organization.** In an individuated society, the act of networking through digital media doesn't just support a movement; it _constitutes_ it.

### Beyond the "Tool" Metaphor

When we look at modern social movements, we often make the mistake of seeing platforms like Signal, Mastodon, or X as mere megaphones. Under the Bennett and Segerberg framework, these platforms provide the **technical affordances**â€”the specific functional propertiesâ€”that replace the need for a physical office or a board of directors.

Consider the **hashtag**. In a traditional organization, a "resource manager" would be responsible for filing documents and ensuring everyone is on the same page. In a digitally networked movement, the hashtag performs this organizational labor automatically. It archives information, creates a searchable public record, and allows disparate individuals to "find" the movement without a formal invitation. The "code" of the platform acts as the "bylaws" of the group.

### The Self-Organizing Property of Networks

Digital networks possess a **self-organizing property** that allows for sophisticated coordination without a legal entity. This is achieved through three key organizational functions:

1. **Resource Allocation:** In the past, an organization decided who got the microphone. Today, "engagement affordances" (likes, shares, and boosts) allocate the "resource" of attention. The crowd collectively decides which ideas are "canon" by amplifying them, creating a real-time meritocracy of information.

2. **Responsiveness:** Because these networks are "always on," they can pivot faster than any committee. If a protest location is blocked, a single pinned post or a geofenced alert re-organizes the entire crowd in seconds. The communication flow _is_ the command structure.

3. **Persistence:** Organizations used to die when they ran out of money. Digital movements, however, can go "dormant" in the code. A network of followers remains connected through their shared digital space, ready to be reactivated by a new event. The organization exists as a latent potential within the network.

### Affordances as Organizational Rules

| Affordance | Traditional Organizational Equivalent | Function in Connective Action |
|---|---|---|
| **Hashtags** | Filing System / Branding | Topic aggregation and identity marking |
| **@Mentions** | Internal Memo / Direct Mail | Directing attention and peer-to-peer accountability |
| **Algorithms** | Executive Committee | Prioritizing information and "surfacing" urgency |
| **Encryption** | Closed-Door Meeting | Creating "safe spaces" for planning and security |

### The "Logic" of the Network

This shift means that "organization" is no longer a noun (a thing you join) but a **verb** (a process you participate in). When you engage with a personalized action frameâ€”sharing a story about your own experience with digital privacy or student debtâ€”you are performing an organizational act. You are adding a "node" to the network and strengthening the structure.

This "communication-as-organization" model is particularly potent for the **DIY citizen**. It removes the high "transaction costs" of traditional activism. You don't need to attend a 7:00 PM meeting in a church basement to be "organized"; you simply need to contribute to the digital flow.
